## 1. What kinds of behaviour cause sexual harassment?

Sexual harassment is any behaviour of a sexual nature that is unwanted and makes someone feel uncomfortable, embarrassed, unsafe, or disrespected. It doesn’t always have to be something extreme — even small actions can count if they are unwelcome.

This includes inappropriate touching, standing too close intentionally, making sexual jokes or comments, commenting on someone’s body or appearance in a suggestive way, repeatedly asking someone out after they’ve said no, or sending inappropriate messages, images, or memes. Staring in a way that makes someone uncomfortable or spreading rumours about someone’s personal life also falls under harassment.

Even if someone says they were “just joking,” it is not acceptable if the other person feels uncomfortable. The key factor is whether the behaviour is unwanted and creates a hostile, intimidating, or unsafe environment.

## 2. What would you do if you face or witness such behaviour?

If I experience such behaviour, I would clearly tell the person to stop, if I feel safe doing so. Sometimes people may not realise the impact of their actions. If the behaviour continues or is serious, I would document everything — including dates, details of what happened, and names of any witnesses — and report it to HR or the Internal Complaints Committee (ICC) according to the organisation’s policy.

If I witness such behaviour happening to someone else, I would not ignore it. I would check in with the person affected and offer support. If they are comfortable, I would encourage them to report the incident and, if necessary, be willing to stand as a witness. Creating a safe and respectful workplace is everyone’s responsibility, and speaking up helps prevent such behaviour from continuing.
